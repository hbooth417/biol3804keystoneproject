About Project

    The animal kingdom contains a tremendous amount of diversity. Diversity exists in all different forms including physiological characteristics, biological processes, and the behaviours of individuals and species. All animal species have behaviours that are similar to those seen in humans, such as selfishness, cooperation, aggression, and altruism. There is also diversity in reproductive and courtship behaviours. Animal courtship behaviours occur when individuals are interested in reproducing. Specific rituals or behaviour are preformed as signals to the opposite sex that illustrate, they are interested in mating. In other words, courtship rituals in animals is a way of impressing the opposite sex in hopes that they will mate with them.

    Diversity of courtship can exist in types of displays, such as a aesthetic showing of bright colours, attractive movements or vocalizations, or displays of aggression or strength. The diversity in courtship behaviours can also stem from the individual(s) who display them. In some species the male participates in the courtship displays, however in others, it is the female who tries to woo the male. There are also species where both males and females put on the mating display.

    The diversity of courtship behaviours in the animal kingdom, can be broadly compared to the mating behaviours of humans. With some exceptions of course! In humans, the goal of courtship and copulation is not always to reproduce. Human relationships are based on trust, respect, friendship, honestly, and many other qualities that animals donâ€™t innately possess. The unique cognitive abilities of humans allow us to have complex relationships with other people, making our courtship styles more complex than multiple species combined.

In this interactive website, we will explore some interesting questions surrounding the diversity of courtship rituals and reproductive behaviours. Diving into the question: Why is courtship so diverse? and Are there mechanisms that explain why these different types of behaviours have evolved? We will also look at different interesting examples of courtship styles in the animal kingdom, and compare them to those found in humans as an added bonus.
